PDFs are the universal document format, but they are not always practical. A scanned insurance policy might be 25 MB. A photo-heavy report could exceed 50 MB. Most email providers cap attachments at 20-25 MB, and uploading large files over cellular data is painfully slow.

The obvious solution is compression — but most people reach for online tools that upload their documents to remote servers. If your PDF contains tax returns, medical records, legal contracts, or any other sensitive content, that is a significant privacy risk.

There is a better way: compress PDF files locally on your device, keeping your documents private while reducing file sizes by 50-90%.

## Why PDF Files Get So Large

Understanding why PDFs balloon in size helps you make better decisions about compression:

- **Scanned documents** are essentially images wrapped in a PDF container. A single scanned page at 300 DPI can be 3-5 MB
- **Embedded images** in reports and presentations are often stored at their original resolution, even when displayed small
- **Embedded fonts** add size, especially when a document uses many typefaces
- **Metadata and layers** from editing software (especially Adobe products) add hidden bulk
- **Redundant objects** accumulate when PDFs are edited and re-saved repeatedly

## The Privacy Angle: Why Local Compression Matters

When you use an online PDF compressor, you upload your document to someone else's server. Even services that promise to delete files after processing still have temporary access to your data. Their privacy policies can change, their servers can be breached, and their employees may have access.

Local compression eliminates this entire risk category. Your document never leaves your device. This is especially important for:

- Financial documents (tax returns, bank statements)
- Medical records (lab results, prescriptions)
- Legal files (contracts, wills, court documents)
- Personal identification (passport scans, driver's license copies)

## Tips for Keeping PDF Sizes Small from the Start

- **Scan at appropriate resolution** — 200 DPI is sufficient for most documents; 300 DPI only if you need to print at high quality
- **Use black and white scanning** for text-only documents instead of color
- **Crop pages** before scanning to remove unnecessary margins
- **Remove unnecessary pages** before compressing — no point in compressing pages you do not need
- **Combine then compress** — merge multiple small PDFs into one, then compress the combined file once

With the right tools and habits, you can keep your document workflow efficient, your file sizes manageable, and your private information secure.
