Weather apps are among the most commonly used apps on any smartphone. They are also among the worst offenders when it comes to privacy. Major weather apps have been caught selling location data to advertisers, sharing information with data brokers, and running background location tracking that drains your battery while monitoring your movements throughout the day.

The weather forecast should not cost you your privacy. Here is why most weather apps are problematic and how to get accurate forecasts without the surveillance.

## The Privacy Problem with Weather Apps

A 2019 investigation by The New York Times revealed that popular weather apps were among the biggest sources of location data sold to third parties. The situation has not improved much since. Here is what many weather apps do:

**Continuous location tracking.** Many weather apps request "Always" location access and use it to track your movements even when the app is closed. This data is far more granular than what is needed for a forecast.

**Third-party ad networks.** Free weather apps typically serve ads through networks that build profiles based on your location, browsing habits, and device identifiers.

**Data broker partnerships.** Your location data is often sold to data brokers who aggregate it with other data sources to build detailed profiles about your habits, commute patterns, and visited locations.

**Dark patterns.** Some apps make it deliberately confusing to deny location permissions or opt out of tracking, using interface tricks that steer you toward sharing more data.

## What a Weather App Actually Needs

To provide an accurate forecast, a weather app needs one thing: your approximate location. Not your continuous GPS coordinates, not your location history, and not background location access. A single location request when you open the app is sufficient.

This is the fundamental mismatch: weather apps request far more data than they need for their stated purpose. The excess data collection exists to serve advertising and data monetization — not to improve your forecast.

## How to Audit Your Current Weather App

If you are not ready to switch, at least audit what your current weather app is doing:

### Check Location Permissions

Go to **Settings > Privacy & Security > Location Services** on your iPhone. Find your weather app and check its access level:

- **Never** — the app cannot access your location (you may need to enter a location manually)
- **While Using** — the app only gets location data when it is open and visible
- **Always** — the app can track your location in the background at any time

If your weather app has "Always" access, change it to "While Using" immediately. There is no legitimate reason a weather app needs background location access.

### Check App Privacy Labels

On the App Store page for any app, scroll down to the "App Privacy" section. Apple requires developers to disclose what data they collect and whether it is linked to your identity. Compare what your current weather app reports versus what YAWA reports.

### Review Background Activity

Go to **Settings > General > Background App Refresh** and disable it for weather apps that do not need to push notifications. This prevents them from waking up in the background to phone home.
