Emergency preparedness is not about paranoia — it is about practical readiness. Natural disasters, severe weather events, power outages, and outdoor emergencies happen to ordinary people in ordinary circumstances. A winter storm knocks out power for five days. A hiking trip goes wrong when the trail is washed out. A hurricane forces evacuation with 24 hours notice.

The people who handle these situations calmly are not survivalist extremists. They are people who took the time to learn a few fundamental skills and prepare basic supplies. This guide covers the essential survival skills that everyone — urban or rural, outdoorsy or not — should know.

## The Five Priorities of Survival

In any emergency situation, address needs in this order:

### 1. Shelter

Exposure kills faster than dehydration or starvation. In cold conditions, hypothermia can set in within hours. In extreme heat, heatstroke is equally dangerous.

**At home during a power outage:**
- Know how to insulate a single room for warmth (close doors, hang blankets over windows, use body heat)
- Keep sleeping bags or heavy blankets accessible, not buried in storage
- Understand how to safely use alternative heat sources (and the carbon monoxide risks)

**Outdoors:**
- Learn to build a basic debris shelter — a lean-to or A-frame using branches and leaves can be constructed in 30-60 minutes
- Carry an emergency space blanket (weighs 2 oz, fits in a pocket, retains 90% of body heat)
- Know how to identify safe shelter locations — away from water, dead trees, and exposed ridgelines

### 2. Water

The human body can survive roughly three days without water, but cognitive and physical decline begins within hours of dehydration.

**Purification methods everyone should know:**
- **Boiling** — the simplest and most reliable method. Bring water to a rolling boil for one minute (three minutes above 6,500 feet elevation).
- **Chemical treatment** — water purification tablets (iodine or chlorine dioxide) are lightweight and effective. Keep a pack in your emergency kit.
- **Filtration** — portable water filters remove bacteria and protozoa. Higher-end models also remove viruses.

**Water storage at home:**
- Keep at least one gallon per person per day for a minimum of three days
- Rotate stored water every six months
- Know where to find emergency water — water heater tank, toilet tank (not bowl), ice cubes

### 3. Fire

Fire provides warmth, water purification, cooking, signaling, and psychological comfort. It is one of the most versatile survival tools.

**Essential fire skills:**
- Practice starting a fire with matches and a lighter in wet conditions — it is harder than it sounds
- Learn to prepare proper tinder (dry grass, birch bark, cotton balls with petroleum jelly) and kindling before attempting ignition
- Understand fire safety — clear the area, contain the fire, never leave it unattended
- Keep waterproof matches or a ferrocerium rod in your emergency kit

### 4. Food

While food is less urgent than shelter, water, and warmth, knowing basic foraging and food preparation skills extends your self-sufficiency.

- **At home:** Maintain a 72-hour supply of non-perishable food that requires no cooking (canned goods, protein bars, dried fruit, peanut butter)
- **Outdoors:** Learn to identify a few reliable edible plants in your region, but never eat anything you cannot positively identify
- **Calorie management:** In a survival situation, minimize exertion to conserve energy

### 5. First Aid

Basic first aid knowledge is arguably the most broadly applicable survival skill.

**Skills everyone should have:**
- Stopping bleeding — direct pressure, pressure points, and tourniquet application
- Treating burns — cool running water, not ice; cover with clean, non-stick dressing
- Recognizing and treating hypothermia and heatstroke
- Splinting a fracture
- CPR and rescue breathing
- Recognizing signs of shock

## Building an Emergency Kit

Every household should have a basic emergency kit. Keep it in an accessible location and check it every six months:

- Water (one gallon per person per day, three-day supply)
- Non-perishable food (three-day supply)
- Flashlight and extra batteries
- First aid kit
- Multi-tool or knife
- Emergency blanket
- Whistle (for signaling)
- Phone charger (battery pack)
- Copies of important documents in a waterproof bag
- Cash in small bills (ATMs may not work during outages)
- Medications (rotation with current prescriptions)
