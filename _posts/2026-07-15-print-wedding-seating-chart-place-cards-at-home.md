Printing your own wedding stationery is one of the few DIY wedding projects that actually saves money without looking homemade, provided you understand what your printer can and cannot do before you hit print. The seating chart, the escort cards, the place cards, and the little table numbers are all just documents. Once the seating is locked and every name is spelled correctly, producing them at home is mostly a matter of choosing the right paper, matching each piece to your printer's real page size, and trimming cleanly.

This guide walks through the whole job the way a careful person would actually do it: deciding what to print, picking paper and weights, matching the poster to your printer's true limits, handling margins and bleed, getting colors close to what you saw on screen, and cutting and folding cards so they stand up straight on the table. Along the way it is honest about the one thing home printers cannot do easily, which is produce a single oversized poster, and it shows you the two clean ways around that.

## Decide what you are actually printing

"Seating stationery" is a bundle of separate pieces, and you do not need all of them. Deciding early keeps the paper order small and the cutting sane.

- **The big seating chart (a poster).** One large sign, usually displayed on an easel near the entrance, that lists every guest and the table they belong to. Guests read it, find their name, and walk to the table. This is the single largest print and the only piece that pushes past a home printer's page size.
- **Escort cards.** Small individual cards, one per guest or per household, laid out alphabetically on a table at the entrance. Each card names the guest and their table number. They do the same job as the poster but as many small pieces instead of one big sheet.
- **Place cards.** Cards that sit at each specific seat and assign a person to that exact chair. These are for seated, plated dinners where you want to control who sits next to whom down to the seat. They are often folded "tent" cards that stand on their own.
- **Table number cards.** Small numbered signs, one per table, so guests can find table 7 once the chart has sent them there.
- **A printed alphabetical guest list.** A plain working document for the coordinator, the caterer, and the venue. Not decorative, but genuinely useful on the day.

Most weddings use either a poster **or** escort cards, not both, plus table numbers. Add place cards only if you are assigning individual seats. If you want the full reasoning on which combination suits your reception, the deep dive on [escort cards vs. place cards vs. seating charts](/blog/productivity/escort-cards-vs-place-cards-vs-seating-charts/) lays out the trade-offs.

The reason this matters for home printing is size. Escort cards, place cards, and table numbers are all small and print comfortably on Letter or A4, several to a page. The poster is the outlier, and everything hard about DIY seating stationery lives in that one piece.

## Match the poster to your printer's real page size

Here is the constraint almost everyone hits: **the vast majority of home inkjet and laser printers max out at Letter (8.5 × 11 in) or A4 (210 × 297 mm).** A seating chart for 120 guests simply does not fit legibly on a sheet that small. Read it from six feet away and the names disappear.

A comfortable seating poster is usually A2 (420 × 594 mm, roughly 16.5 × 23.4 in) or at least A3 or Tabloid (11 × 17 in). Those sizes do not come out of a standard home printer, so you have exactly three honest options.

1. **Tile the poster across many small sheets and assemble it.** You export the poster at full A2 size, run it through a tiling tool that slices it into Letter or A4 pieces with a little overlap, print all the tiles, trim the overlap, and tape or mount them together. This is genuinely how large signs get made at home, and with a matte mount it looks clean.
2. **Send the full-size PDF to a print shop or office store.** You email or upload one A2 PDF and pick it up printed on a single large sheet. No tiling, no assembly, a crisp result, and a modest fee.
3. **Skip the poster entirely and print escort cards instead.** Many small cards on Letter or A4 sidestep the size problem completely, because no single piece is ever larger than a standard sheet.

There is no fourth option where a Letter-size printer quietly produces an A2 poster. Being clear-eyed about this before you design saves a frustrating evening.

### How tiling actually works

Tiling takes one large page and prints it as a grid of overlapping standard sheets. An A2 poster becomes roughly four to six Letter tiles depending on orientation and the overlap you choose. Each tile carries a small margin of shared image so you can line up neighbors precisely, then you trim that overlap off one edge of each sheet and butt the pages together.

## Choose paper and weights

Paper does most of the work in making a home print look intentional rather than improvised. The two variables that matter are weight and finish.

**Weight** is measured in pounds (in the US, by a confusing "basis weight" system) or in gsm (grams per square meter, used almost everywhere else and far easier to reason about). For our purposes:

- **Cards (escort, place, table numbers): around 110–120 lb cover, roughly 300–350 gsm.** This is proper cardstock. It stands up on its own when folded, does not curl, and feels like stationery rather than a printout. Anything lighter than about 80 lb cover (~215 gsm) tends to flop and looks cheap on the table.
- **The poster: around 80–100 lb cover, or a heavy 100 lb text, roughly 200–270 gsm.** A poster gets mounted or framed, so it does not need to be as rigid as a standing card, but it should be substantial enough not to ripple or show through under gallery lighting. If you are mounting the tiles to a foam board, you can go lighter, since the board provides the stiffness.

**Finish** is the other choice:

- **Matte** is the safe default for both posters and cards. It photographs well, avoids glare on the easel, and hides minor inkjet banding. For anything guests will read at a distance, matte is almost always the right call.
- **Uncoated / textured cardstock** (cotton, linen, or laid finishes) reads as elegant and traditional for place and escort cards. It takes ink slightly softer, so fine text can look a touch less crisp, but the tactile quality is worth it for small cards held in the hand.
- **Glossy** is generally the wrong choice here. It glares under lighting and looks more like a photo print than stationery.

Before you commit to a full order, confirm your printer accepts the weight you want. Many home inkjets handle up to about 110 lb cover through a rear or straight-path feed, but a tight front tray can jam on heavy stock. Check the specification and feed heavy cardstock one or a few sheets at a time rather than fanning a full stack.

## Margins, bleed, and why edge-to-edge needs trimming

Two printing concepts trip up first-timers, and both are easy once named.

**Margins.** Home printers cannot print to the very edge of a sheet; there is almost always an unprintable border of a few millimeters. If your design pushes text or a border right to the paper edge, that content gets clipped. The simplest safe approach is to keep important content — every name, every table number — well inside the sheet, with a comfortable margin, so nothing risks being cut off by the printer's own limits.

**Bleed.** If you *want* a design where color or a background runs all the way to the edge of the finished card, you cannot rely on the printer to reach the edge. Instead you print the design slightly larger than the final card — extending the background a few millimeters past where the cut will fall — and then **trim** the sheet down to size. That extra printed margin is the "bleed," and trimming through it guarantees color reaches the true edge with no thin white sliver. This is why professional cards look edge-to-edge and home prints often do not: the pros trim.

For most DIY couples the practical rule is this. If your cards have a plain background with text floating in the middle, you can print them at final size and cut on the design's own crop marks, no bleed needed. If your cards have a full-color background or a border meant to reach the edge, add a few millimeters of bleed and trim. Either way, a design with crop marks or cut guides makes the cutting stage far more accurate than eyeballing it.

## Get the colors right

Screens glow; paper reflects. A color that looks like a soft sage on your display can print as a flatter, grayer green, because the monitor makes color with light and the paper makes it with ink. You will not get a perfect match at home, but you can get close and avoid unpleasant surprises.

- **Print one real test sheet on your real paper** before you commit to the whole run. Colors shift noticeably between plain paper and cardstock, and between matte and textured stock. The test is the single most useful step in the entire process.
- **Favor darker text on light backgrounds.** Black or deep charcoal text on cream or white cardstock is legible, forgiving, and prints consistently. Light text on a dark background is dramatic but eats ink, can look blotchy on an inkjet, and is harder to read at distance on a poster.
- **Turn off "fit to page" scaling when you want exact sizes.** Auto-scaling silently shrinks your document and throws off both dimensions and where cuts should fall. Print at 100% / actual size.
- **Let the printer warm up and check for banding.** If you see faint horizontal stripes, run a nozzle clean before printing the full batch.

## Cut, score, and fold cleanly

Sharp cutting is what separates DIY stationery that looks deliberate from stationery that looks rushed. A pair of scissors will not do it for straight edges.

- **Use a guillotine or a rotary trimmer with a metal edge.** These give you a true straight cut every time and let you cut several sheets of cardstock in one pass if the blade is sharp. A self-healing mat plus a steel ruler and a craft knife is the budget alternative and works well with patience.
- **Cut on the crop marks, not by eye.** If your layout includes cut guides, follow them. Consistency across a hundred cards matters more than absolute precision on any single one; a stack that is all the same size looks professional even if each card is a hair off the theoretical ideal.
- **Score before you fold tent cards.** A tent-fold place card that you simply bend will crack along the fold and lean. Instead, *score* the fold line first — run a bone folder, an empty ballpoint, or the back of a craft knife along a ruler to press a crease into the cardstock without cutting through it — then fold along that scored line. The card folds crisply, stands square, and does not crack the printed surface. On heavier 110–120 lb cover, scoring is not optional; unscored heavy stock always cracks.
- **Fold with the printed side out and the crease sharp.** Run the bone folder along the finished fold to set it. Tent cards that stand at a clean right angle read far better on a place setting than ones that slouch.

## Alphabetical vs. by-table ordering for the big chart

The one design decision that changes how the poster reads is ordering, and the rule is simple: **order by whatever the guest knows.**

- **Alphabetical by last name** is right for the entrance poster and for escort cards. A guest arriving knows their own name, not their table number, so an A-to-Z list lets them scan straight to themselves and read off the table. This is the standard for a reason.
- **By table** is right for the coordinator's working copy, the caterer, and the venue staff, who think in tables ("who is at 7?"). It is the wrong order for a guest-facing sign, because nobody arrives knowing their table.

Print both when it helps: an alphabetical poster and escort cards for guests, plus a by-table alphabetical guest list for the day-of team. ## A realistic cost and time comparison

Neither DIY nor professional printing is universally cheaper; it depends on quantity and on how you value your evenings. Here is an honest sketch.

**Professional / print shop.** You send finished PDFs and pick up trimmed, mounted results. A single large poster printed at an office store is inexpensive and looks flawless with zero labor. A full stationery suite from a wedding printer costs more and takes lead time, but arrives cut, scored, and perfect. The strengths are quality, edge-to-edge bleed done right, and no work on your part. The cost is money and turnaround time.

**DIY at home.** Your outlay is paper, ink, and time. Cardstock is the main material cost, and ink is real — a hundred cards plus a tiled poster will use a meaningful chunk of a cartridge, so budget for that rather than assuming ink is free. The labor is the trimming and folding, which for a hundred cards is a couple of focused hours. DIY wins clearly on small runs, on last-minute changes (a late RSVP is a five-minute reprint, not a reorder), and on total control. It loses on the big poster, where tiling and mounting is more effort than most people want.

A common and sensible split: **print all the small pieces at home** (escort cards, place cards, table numbers, guest list — fast, cheap, easy to reprint) and **send only the one large poster to a shop**, or tile it yourself if you enjoy the craft. That hybrid captures most of the savings and skips the hardest part. ## The pre-print checklist

Do not print anything until you can check every one of these. A single missed step here is what turns a smooth evening into a reprint.

- **Proofread every name, out loud, against a trusted source.** Nicknames, maiden versus married names, accented characters, and juniors versus seniors are where mistakes hide. A misspelled name on a place card is the one printing error a guest will notice and remember.
- **Lock the final seating.** Wait until the RSVP count is truly final. Reprinting because one table changed is annoying; reprinting the whole poster because you jumped early is worse.
- **Confirm each piece's size against your printer.** Cards and table numbers on Letter/A4 print at home. The poster's real size — A2, A3, or Tabloid — determines whether you tile it or send it out. Decide now, not at the printer.
- **Export at full size with scaling off.** Export each PDF at its true dimensions, and when you print, choose actual size / 100%, never "fit to page," so cut marks land where they should.
- **Print one test page on the real paper.** One escort card and, if you are tiling, one poster tile. Check color, legibility, margins, and that cut guides are where you expect before running the full batch.
- **Test the fold and cut on a scrap.** Score and fold one tent card and trim one edge before committing the stack, so you catch a wrong fold line or a mis-set trimmer early.

Run that list, and the actual printing becomes the boring, satisfying part: load the cardstock, hit print, trim, fold, and set out a stationery suite that matches the chart down to the last name.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I print a wedding seating chart poster on a regular home printer?

Not as one big sheet. Almost all home printers stop at Letter or A4, which is far too small for a legible seating poster. You have two real options: tile the full-size poster across several small sheets and assemble them, using a tool like XLPrinter with Soirée's exported PDF, or send the single full-size PDF to a print shop or office store. The escort cards, place cards, and table numbers, by contrast, all print fine at home.

### What paper weight should I use for place cards and escort cards?

Aim for around 110–120 lb cover, roughly 300–350 gsm. That is proper cardstock: it stands up when folded into a tent card, resists curling, and feels like stationery. Lighter stock below about 80 lb cover flops and looks cheap on the table. Check that your printer's feed path accepts heavy cardstock, and feed thick stock a few sheets at a time to avoid jams.

### Why do my cards have a thin white edge when I wanted color to the edge?

Because home printers cannot print all the way to the paper's edge, and cutting exactly on the design line leaves a sliver of unprinted paper. The fix is bleed: print the design slightly larger than the finished card, extending the background a few millimeters past the cut line, then trim through that extra margin. Trimming into the bleed guarantees color reaches the true edge with no white sliver.

### Should the seating chart be alphabetical or by table?

Order it by whatever the guest already knows. For the entrance poster and escort cards, that is alphabetical by last name, so an arriving guest can scan to their own name and read off their table. Order by table only for the coordinator, caterer, and venue staff, who think in tables. Many couples print an alphabetical guest-facing chart and a separate by-table list for the day-of team; Soirée exports both from the same locked seating.

### How do I fold tent place cards so they stand up straight?

Score the fold line before folding. Run a bone folder, an empty ballpoint, or the back of a craft knife along a ruler to press a crease into the cardstock without cutting through it, then fold along that scored line and set the crease with the bone folder. Heavy 110–120 lb cover always cracks if you fold it without scoring first. Soirée builds the fold line into its tent-card layout so you know exactly where to score.

### Is it cheaper to print wedding stationery at home or use a print shop?

It depends on quantity and on the piece. Small items — escort cards, place cards, table numbers — are cheaper and faster at home, and a late RSVP becomes a five-minute reprint rather than a reorder. The large poster is the exception: tiling and mounting it is real effort, so many couples send only the poster to a shop and print everything else at home. Budget for ink, which is a genuine cost on a full run, when comparing.

### What is the single most important step before printing everything?

Print one test page on your actual paper. Colors shift between screen and paper and between plain paper and cardstock, margins can clip content, and cut guides need to land in the right place. One test card, and one poster tile if you are tiling, catches nearly every problem before you have committed a full batch of expensive cardstock. Pair that with proofreading every name and locking the final seating, and the rest of the job is routine.
