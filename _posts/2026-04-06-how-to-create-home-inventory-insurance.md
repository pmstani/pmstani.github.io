If a fire, flood, or burglary struck your home today, could you list everything you own from memory? Most people cannot. And without documentation, filing an insurance claim becomes a frustrating exercise in guesswork — often resulting in significantly lower payouts than you deserve.

A home inventory solves this problem. It is a detailed record of your belongings, complete with photos, descriptions, serial numbers, and estimated values. Creating one takes a few hours, but it can save you thousands of dollars when you need it most.

## Why a Home Inventory Matters

Insurance adjusters need proof of what you owned and what it was worth. Without documentation:

- **You will forget items.** Studies show that people recall only 30-40% of their possessions from memory after a loss event.
- **Claims get undervalued.** Without receipts or photos, the adjuster estimates replacement costs conservatively.
- **Disputes drag on.** Undocumented claims take longer to process and are more likely to be contested.
- **Special items get missed.** Jewelry, electronics, collectibles, and tools are easy to overlook but add up quickly.

A thorough home inventory typically increases insurance claim payouts by 20-50% compared to claims filed from memory alone.

## How to Build Your Home Inventory: Step by Step

### Step 1: Choose Your Tools

You need two things: a way to catalog items with details, and a secure place to store the inventory itself.

[Equipt](/apps/equipt/) is an asset tracking app that lets you log items with photos, serial numbers, purchase dates, warranty information, and custom notes. It is designed for exactly this kind of cataloging work.

For secure storage of receipts, warranty documents, and other supporting files, [Safe](/apps/safe/) provides encrypted, on-device document storage. Together, these apps give you a complete system.

### Step 2: Work Room by Room

Do not try to inventory your entire home in one sitting. Work through one room at a time:

**Living Room.** Television, sound system, gaming consoles, furniture, artwork, decorative items, rugs, lamps.

**Kitchen.** Appliances (especially the expensive ones — stand mixer, espresso machine, food processor), cookware sets, knife sets, small electronics.

**Bedrooms.** Furniture, mattresses, clothing (estimate total value per person), jewelry, electronics, books.

**Home Office.** Computer, monitor, printer, desk, chair, software licenses, external drives, peripherals.

**Garage / Storage.** Power tools, hand tools, lawn equipment, bicycles, sports gear, seasonal decorations, camping equipment.

**Bathroom.** While individual items are low-value, high-end toiletries, electric razors, hair tools, and towel sets add up.

### Step 3: Document Each Item

For every item worth more than $50, record:

- **Description** — make, model, color, distinguishing features
- **Serial number** — found on electronics, appliances, and tools
- **Purchase date** — approximate is fine if you do not have the exact date
- **Purchase price** — or estimated current replacement cost
- **Photo** — clear, well-lit image showing the item and any identifying marks
- **Receipt or proof of purchase** — store digital copies in Safe for encrypted safekeeping

### Step 4: Calculate Estimated Values

As you catalog items, keep a running total by room and by category. This helps you verify that your insurance coverage limits are adequate. Many people discover during this process that their policy limits are too low for their actual possessions.

### Step 5: Store Your Inventory Securely

Your inventory is useless if it is destroyed in the same event that damages your belongings. Store it in at least two locations:

- On your phone (via Equipt)
- In an encrypted backup (via Safe or an encrypted cloud backup)
- Optionally, share a copy with a trusted family member

### Step 6: Update Regularly

Schedule a review every six months or whenever you make a major purchase. Add new items and remove things you have sold or discarded.

## What to Prioritize If You Are Short on Time

If a full inventory feels overwhelming, start with the highest-value categories:

1. **Electronics** — phones, computers, tablets, TVs, gaming systems
2. **Jewelry and watches** — often underinsured and hard to prove without documentation
3. **Appliances** — refrigerator, washer, dryer, dishwasher, oven
4. **Tools and equipment** — power tools, workshop equipment, lawn care
5. **Furniture** — beds, sofas, dining sets, desks

Even a partial inventory focused on high-value items is dramatically better than no inventory at all.
