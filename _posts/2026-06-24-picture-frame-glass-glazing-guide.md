# Picture Frame Glass Guide: Conservation, Museum and Anti-Reflective Glazing

Glazing is the single most misunderstood decision in a custom framing order. The moulding gets all the attention because it is the part the customer can hold; the mat board gets discussed because the colour is visible. But the sheet of glass — or acrylic — in front of the artwork does more to determine how the piece looks under real light, how long it survives, and how much the order costs than almost any other choice. It is also the easiest place to either under-protect a valuable piece or over-spend on a disposable one.

This guide is for two audiences at once. If you run a frame shop, it is a reference for recommending the right glazing tier honestly and pricing it correctly. If you are a customer trying to decide between the "regular" option and the one that costs three times as much, it explains exactly what you are paying for. The physics is the same for both. Good glazing advice is just accurate physics, applied to a specific piece on a specific wall.

## Why glazing matters more than people think

A sheet of framing glass is doing four jobs simultaneously, and they pull in different directions.

- **Physical protection.** Glazing is a barrier against fingerprints, dust, airborne grime, insects, and the casual knock. For anything on paper, it also holds the work flat and keeps it from cockling against the inside of the frame. This is the job everyone assumes glass is doing — and the only one a basic clear sheet does well.
- **UV protection.** Ultraviolet light, from both daylight and many indoor sources, breaks down the organic dyes and pigments in artwork. The damage is **cumulative and irreversible.** A watercolour that has faded does not come back. This is where glazing tiers genuinely diverge, and where the wrong choice is silently destructive.
- **Reflection and readability.** Ordinary glass reflects roughly 8% of the light hitting it (about 4% off each surface). On a dark image, or a piece hung opposite a window, those reflections can make the art nearly impossible to see. Reducing reflection is the second axis on which premium glazing competes.
- **Weight and safety.** Glass is heavy and breaks into sharp pieces. Above a certain size, or in a child's room, over a bed, or up a staircase, weight and breakage stop being abstract and become a real reason to choose acrylic instead.

No single product is best at all four. The art of recommending glazing is matching the piece and its environment to the trade-offs, not reflexively selling the most expensive sheet in the rack.

## The main glazing tiers

Framing glazing sorts into three meaningful tiers, plus one older option that still has a place. Understanding what each one actually does — and does not do — is the whole game.

### 1. Regular (clear) glass

This is standard float glass: soda-lime glass drawn flat on a bath of molten tin, the same basic material as a window pane, cut and cleaned for framing. It is inexpensive, optically clear enough for most everyday work, and widely available.

What it does: provides full physical protection and holds the piece flat.

What it does **not** do: it offers essentially no meaningful UV filtering, and it reflects light like any uncoated glass. A small amount of UV is absorbed by ordinary glass, but not enough to call it protection — the standard figure of merit for conservation glazing is blocking roughly 99% of UV, and clear float glass is nowhere near that.

Use it for: posters, prints you can reprint, temporary displays, low-value decorative pieces, and anything you genuinely expect to replace. There is nothing wrong with regular glass on a $12 poster. There is a great deal wrong with it on an original.

### 2. Conservation / UV-filtering glass

Conservation glass is float glass with a coating (or a laminated interlayer, depending on the product) that absorbs ultraviolet light. The widely cited specification is that it filters approximately **99% of UV radiation** across the damaging wavelengths. Figures vary by product and should be treated as approximate, but the category exists to do one job extremely well: slow down fading.

What it adds over regular glass: the UV barrier. That is the entire point. It does not noticeably reduce reflections — a standard UV-filtering sheet looks much like clear glass and reflects much like it too.

Important honesty for both shop and customer: **UV filtering slows fading; it does not stop it.** Visible light still carries energy, and even with 99% UV blocked, intense or prolonged light exposure will eventually affect fugitive pigments. Conservation glazing buys time — often a great deal of it — but the right advice for a precious piece is still to keep it off a sun-blasted wall.

Use it for: anything you want to keep. Original art on paper, watercolours, photographs, archival prints, signed editions, certificates, documents, textiles. If a piece has sentimental or monetary value and you would be upset to see it fade, conservation glazing is the baseline, not the upgrade.

### 3. Anti-reflective / museum glass

This is the top tier. Museum glass combines a UV-filtering substrate with a multi-layer optical anti-reflective coating on both faces — the same family of coating technology used on quality camera lenses and eyeglasses. The coating suppresses surface reflection dramatically, taking the visible reflectance down to roughly 1% or less, compared with about 8% for uncoated glass.

The effect in person is striking. Properly coated museum glass can look as if there is no glass in the frame at all. Dark images read fully, the piece keeps its contrast and depth, and a frame hung opposite a window stops mirroring the room. Because the best products pair this coating with the conservation-grade UV filter, you get near-invisibility **and** ~99% UV protection in one sheet.

What it costs: this is the most expensive glazing by a wide margin, often several times the price of conservation glass for the same size. The coatings also benefit from careful handling and cleaning — they are durable in normal use, but they are a precision optical surface, not a windowpane.

Use it for: fine art, dark or high-contrast images, anything going onto a difficult wall with strong reflections, gallery and exhibition pieces, and any job where the customer's reaction to seeing the proof under glass is part of the sale. When someone leans in to a framed piece and asks "is there even glass in there?", that is museum glass earning its price.

### A note on anti-glare / etched glass (the older option)

Separate from the three tiers above sits anti-glare glass, sometimes called non-glare or etched glass. This is older technology: one surface is acid-etched or mechanically frosted so that it diffuses reflections by scattering them rather than suppressing them with a coating.

The trade-off is real and worth explaining plainly. Anti-glare glass does reduce mirror reflections, but it does so by softening the entire image — it can look slightly hazy or "milky," and the effect worsens the further the glass sits from the artwork (which is exactly the situation with deep mats or shadow boxes). It is much cheaper than coated museum glass, and many products are available with or without UV filtering.

It still has a place: large pieces in bright rooms where coated glass is out of budget, and situations where reflection is the dominant complaint and a little softness is acceptable. But it is not a substitute for true anti-reflective coating. If a customer wants both clarity and no reflections, museum glass is the honest answer; anti-glare is the compromise.

## Glass versus acrylic

Glass is not always the right material at all. Acrylic glazing — sold under names like Plexiglas, and in conservation grades such as OP-3 — is a clear plastic sheet that replaces glass in specific situations.

When acrylic is the better choice:

- **Large pieces.** Glass weight rises fast with area, and large sheets are fragile and dangerous to handle and hang. Acrylic is roughly half the weight of glass for the same size, which makes oversized framing safer and easier to mount.
- **Shipping and transport.** Glass and the postal system are enemies. If a framed piece is going in a box — to a customer, to a show, between galleries — acrylic dramatically reduces the risk of arriving as a bag of shards.
- **Children's rooms, over beds, high or public places.** Anywhere a break would be a hazard to a person, shatter-resistant acrylic is the responsible call. It does not break into sharp pieces.
- **UV protection in plastic form.** UV-filtering acrylic exists (OP-3 and similar conservation grades block roughly 98–99% of UV), so choosing acrylic does not mean giving up conservation-level protection.

The caveats, stated honestly:

- **Scratching.** Acrylic is softer than glass and scratches more easily. It should be cleaned only with a soft cloth and an acrylic-safe cleaner — never paper towel, never ammonia-based glass cleaner, which can damage the surface.
- **Static.** Acrylic carries a static charge, which can lift loose pigment, pastel, charcoal, or chalk off the surface of the artwork. For friable media — pastels, soft charcoal, unfixed chalk — acrylic is the wrong choice, and glass is preferred. Anti-static acrylic grades exist but add cost.

A simple rule of thumb: glass for clarity, scratch resistance, and friable media; acrylic for size, weight, breakage risk, and anything that has to travel.

## Spacing the glazing off the art

Whatever the material, one principle holds for any piece with conservation intent: **glazing should never touch the surface of the artwork.** Direct contact causes several distinct problems — condensation can form between glass and art and breed mould; inks and emulsions can stick to the glass over time; and certain media transfer onto the glass surface and are then permanently damaged.

There are two standard ways to hold the glazing off the art:

- **Matting.** A window mat does double duty: it is the visible border, and its thickness lifts the glass off the artwork. This is one more reason mats are a conservation feature and not just a decorative one — a subject explored in depth in the [single vs double mat custom framing guide](/blog/productivity/single-vs-double-mat-custom-framing-guide/).
- **Spacers.** When a piece is framed without a mat — a "close-frame" look, or a canvas behind glass — thin spacers are tucked under the rabbet to create the same gap invisibly. Spacers are also what you reach for in a deep shadow box.

The takeaway for both shop and customer: if a quote includes conservation glazing but the design puts glass flat against the art, the protection is undermined. Glazing tier and spacing go together.

## How to recommend glazing by use case

The right glazing is a function of two things: the value of the piece, and the light it will live in. Walk through the order with that frame of mind and the recommendation almost makes itself.

- **A cheap poster.** Regular clear glass. It is replaceable, it is not precious, and spending on conservation glazing is money wasted. Honesty here builds trust for the next order.
- **An original watercolour or a signed photographic print.** Conservation glass at minimum — fugitive pigments and dyes fade, and the loss is permanent. If it is going somewhere bright, conservation is non-negotiable, and museum glass is a reasonable upgrade for clarity.
- **A dark, high-contrast image — a charcoal drawing, a moody photograph, an oil reproduction.** Museum glass. Dark images are where reflections are most destructive to readability, and where anti-reflective coating most obviously transforms the piece. This is the easiest museum-glass sale to make because the proof speaks for itself.
- **A sports jersey or memorabilia shadow box.** Conservation glazing for the UV protection (fabric and printed graphics fade), and acrylic if the box is large or the piece will be moved — shadow boxes are heavy, and acrylic keeps the weight and breakage risk down. Spacers are mandatory here regardless.
- **A piece going onto a sunny wall.** This is the highest-stakes case. Conservation glazing at minimum, and a frank conversation that even 99% UV filtering only slows the damage — direct sun is the enemy. For anything irreplaceable on a sunny wall, the best advice may be to suggest a different wall.

The recommendation is rarely "buy the most expensive option." It is "match the glass to what this piece is and where it's going." Customers trust a framer who talks them down from an upgrade as readily as up to one.

## The pricing impact: glazing is a major lever

Here is the part that matters at the counter. **Glazing tier is one of the largest single levers on a framing quote.** Because glass is priced by area — the same united-inch or square-area logic that drives the rest of the estimate, covered in the [custom frame pricing guide](/blog/productivity/how-to-price-custom-picture-frame/) — moving from regular glass to museum glass can swing the total by a meaningful fraction, especially on larger pieces.

Consider a single frame, with the figures kept deliberately approximate to illustrate the mechanism rather than quote real prices:

- **Regular glass:** the baseline. Cheapest glazing, minimal effect on the total.
- **Conservation glass:** often two to three times the glass cost of regular. On a mid-sized piece this is a noticeable but easy-to-justify bump — the customer is buying permanence.
- **Museum glass:** frequently four to six times the conservation cost, and the single biggest line on many premium quotes. On a large piece the glazing upgrade alone can rival the moulding cost.

Because glass cost scales with area, the upgrade penalty grows with size. A glazing tier that adds a modest amount on an 8×10 can add a large amount on a 30×40. That is exactly why showing the price change live, as the customer chooses, is so powerful — it lets them weigh clarity and protection against cost with the real number in front of them, rather than discovering it on the final invoice. ## Bringing it together

Glazing is where a framing order quietly succeeds or fails. Get it right and a piece looks its best and lasts for decades; get it wrong and you either waste a customer's money or watch an irreplaceable work fade behind glass that was never going to protect it. The decision is not complicated once you hold the four jobs — protection, UV, reflection, weight — in mind and match the tier to the piece and its wall.

Regular glass for the disposable. Conservation glass as the honest baseline for anything you want to keep. Museum glass when clarity and reflection matter and the value justifies it. Acrylic when size, transport, or safety make glass the wrong material. Spacers or a mat so the glazing never touches the art. And a clear, live view of what each choice costs, so the customer chooses with open eyes. That last part is exactly the gap a tool like Mitre fills — turning a confusing upsell into a visible, honest comparison the customer can make for themselves.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is conservation glass worth the extra cost?

For anything you would be upset to see fade — original art, photographs, certificates, anything with sentimental or monetary value — yes. UV damage is cumulative and irreversible, and conservation glazing's roughly 99% UV filtering buys you decades you cannot get back once a piece has faded. For a replaceable poster, no — regular glass is the honest choice.

### Does museum glass really make the glass invisible?

Close to it. The multi-layer anti-reflective coating drops surface reflectance from around 8% to roughly 1% or less, so on most pieces — especially dark or high-contrast images — it can genuinely look as if there is no glass in the frame. It is the most expensive glazing by a wide margin, and the proof under real light is what justifies the price.

### What is the difference between anti-glare glass and museum glass?

Anti-glare (etched/non-glare) glass scatters reflections by frosting one surface, which softens the whole image and can look slightly hazy, especially when the glass sits far from the art. Museum glass suppresses reflections with an optical coating, staying perfectly clear. Anti-glare is cheaper; museum glass is the choice when you want both no reflections and no loss of clarity.

### Should I choose glass or acrylic?

Choose acrylic for large pieces, anything being shipped, children's rooms, spots over beds, and high or public places — it is about half the weight and shatter-resistant, and UV-filtering grades like OP-3 exist. Choose glass for the best clarity and scratch resistance, and always for friable media like pastel or charcoal, because acrylic's static charge can lift loose pigment off the surface.

### Why shouldn't the glass touch the artwork?

Direct contact can trap condensation and cause mould, cause inks or emulsions to stick to the glass over time, and damage media that transfers onto the surface. A window mat or thin spacers hold the glazing off the art, which is one reason matting is a conservation feature, not just a decorative one.

### Does UV-filtering glass stop fading completely?

No. It filters roughly 99% of ultraviolet light, which dramatically slows fading, but visible light still carries energy and will eventually affect fugitive pigments under intense or prolonged exposure. Conservation glazing buys time; the best protection for an irreplaceable piece is still to keep it out of direct sunlight.

### How much does upgrading glazing change the price of a frame?

A lot, and more on larger pieces, because glass is priced by area. Conservation glass often runs two to three times the cost of regular glass, and museum glass several times the cost of conservation — on a large piece the glazing upgrade alone can rival the moulding cost. Showing the price update live as the tier changes lets the customer weigh protection and clarity against cost with the real number in front of them.
