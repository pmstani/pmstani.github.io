Staying current with technology news is essential for developers, product managers, and anyone working in tech. But the modern information landscape makes it surprisingly hard to do well. Social media feeds are optimized for engagement, not information quality. Traditional news sites bury technical content under clickbait. And the sheer volume of content published daily makes it impossible to read everything.

The solution is a curated reading system — a set of tools and sources that surface the most relevant content efficiently, without requiring you to scroll through noise.

## The Problem with Default News Consumption

Most people consume tech news through one of these channels:

**Social media (Twitter/X, Reddit, LinkedIn).** Algorithmically sorted feeds prioritize viral content over substantive reporting. You see what generates reactions, not what is most informative. The signal-to-noise ratio is poor.

**General news sites.** Mainstream tech coverage tends toward product announcements, funding rounds, and executive drama. Deep technical content is rare.

**Email newsletters.** Useful but asynchronous. By the time you read a weekly digest, the most time-sensitive information may be days old.

**Random browsing.** Opening a dozen tabs and reading whatever catches your eye is time-consuming and biased toward whatever you happen to encounter first.

## A Better Approach: Curated Sources and Dedicated Readers

### Hacker News: Community-Curated Quality

Hacker News (news.ycombinator.com) is a link aggregation site run by Y Combinator. Its community upvotes technical content, research papers, programming tools, and thoughtful essays. The content that reaches the front page has been filtered by a technically literate audience, which makes it one of the highest signal-to-noise tech news sources available.

However, the default Hacker News website is bare-bones. A dedicated reader app makes the experience significantly better.

[Tech News Pro](/apps/tech-news-pro/) is a Hacker News reader designed for mobile consumption. It provides a clean reading experience with features that the default site lacks:

- **Clean article rendering** — read linked articles without web clutter
- **Comment threading** — Hacker News discussions are often as valuable as the linked articles
- **Offline reading** — save articles for reading without an internet connection
- **Filtering** — focus on the topics most relevant to your work

### RSS Feeds: Your Personal News Wire

RSS (Really Simple Syndication) allows you to subscribe to websites and receive updates whenever new content is published. Unlike social media algorithms, RSS shows you everything from your selected sources in chronological order — nothing hidden, nothing promoted, nothing injected.

RSS is particularly powerful for following:

- **Technical blogs** by individual developers and companies
- **Release notes** for frameworks and tools you depend on
- **Research labs** publishing new papers (Google AI, OpenAI, DeepMind)
- **Niche publications** that cover your specific technology stack

[RSS Reader](/apps/rss-reader/) provides a streamlined mobile RSS experience. Add feeds for your essential sources and check them in a single unified timeline.

### Recommended RSS Feeds for Developers

Here are high-quality feeds to get started with:

**General tech:**
- Ars Technica — in-depth technical reporting
- The Verge — product and industry coverage
- Hacker News RSS — top stories from HN

**Programming:**
- Dev.to — community-written technical articles
- CSS-Tricks — web development techniques
- The Pragmatic Engineer — software engineering industry analysis

**Specific languages and frameworks:**
- Official blogs for your primary language (Swift, Rust, Python, Go, etc.)
- Framework blogs (React, Django, Rails, etc.)
- GitHub Trending — popular new repositories

## Building an Efficient Reading Routine

Having the right tools is only half the solution. You also need a sustainable routine:

**Set time boundaries.** Allocate 15-20 minutes in the morning and optionally 10 minutes after lunch for tech reading. Do not let it expand to fill your entire break.

**Triage ruthlessly.** Scan headlines first. Open only the articles that are directly relevant to your work or genuinely interesting. Most headlines can be dismissed at a glance.

**Save for later, do not context-switch.** When you find a long article during work hours, save it for your dedicated reading time rather than reading it immediately.

**Rotate your focus.** You do not need to follow everything at once. This month, focus on frontend developments. Next month, dive into infrastructure. Rotating your attention prevents overwhelm while maintaining broad awareness.

**Unsubscribe aggressively.** If a source consistently fails to provide value, remove it. A lean feed with ten high-quality sources beats a bloated one with fifty mediocre sources.
